Intertextual Conversation

Zaccaria Scarpa · ENC 3416 · April 27, 2026

An ePortfolio isn't just a gallery to show off your finished works. It is a rhetorical argument about your identity as a writer and thinker, and the decisions made throughout constructing it are rhetorical even if that is not your goal. Now that I have looked back at my three works I will be including in my ePortfolio — that being a journalism-style magazine spread about the Seattle Seahawks, an interview with Hank Schrader from Breaking Bad formatted as a nonfiction newspaper article "fairytale" retelling, and a key terms mind map of my digital rhetoric course reading annotations — I can see that each piece is representing the end goal of this course of digital writing, audience, and writer identity. Three of our course texts that I have already written deeply about their connections and context helped me to think about these choices: Craig's paper on ePortfolios as exhibits, Babb's work on social media as epideictic discourse, and Yancey's outlook on reflective transfer.

Craig — The Digital Exhibit

In Craig's work, he explains that digital portfolios are different from paper because they aren't just print portfolios online, but build off of them by using new strategies that the digital age created. In his model, a student like Maria does not prove her argument through straight evidence. She instead creates an exhibit where the viewer can make their own connections. The model he described made me ask why we were assigned to make a magazine spread. Its purpose was not meant to be read as an essay. Its makeup is straight from a sports magazine with pull quotes, section heads, and more of a focus on visual elements because that is what the assignment was trying to teach us about structuring arguments in different digital models. That I can work in an out of norm professional genre and format information on the page to fit the unfamiliar style. Craig would see that as the type of evidence by curation that he is talking about. The article doesn't tell you that I know what I'm talking about with sports journalism; it simply proves through intertextuality to the reader that I do.

As Craig describes it, digital platforms allow students the "opportunity to collect research in a wider range of environments," and the spread does that by letting the arrangement of the piece do the rhetorical work (Craig 2).

Babb — Epideictic Discourse and Identity

While Craig mentions what digital platforms make possible, Babb mentions what they require. He claims that social media is epideictic discourse — speech focused on the present moment that creates identity through public performance and audience response. Any post is an act of rhetoric that is judged by an audience, and that audience is part of what goes into creating the post, sometimes outside of what the author intended. His case study of Walter Palmer, who had his public reputation ruined by an online audience he hadn't directly engaged, reminds us that online writing is always a communicative act.

When I read the Hank Schrader article in this context, it has more significance. The piece is a facade; Hank Schrader obviously is a fictional character and supposed to be giving the interview post-mortem, but it is a valid newspaper article. It performs authenticity within a genre. The assignment was structured this way to demonstrate that I could write something that would be up to the standard of journalism, but the subject matter is fiction. Babb would argue that genre performance is rhetorical and that it is done only because there is a discourse community that knows what an interview in the newspaper should sound like. My article draws on that credibility, and Babb's claims explain why that borrowing is not only stylistic, but also a claim about identity and membership in a discourse community (Babb 9).

Yancey — Reflective Transfer

Yancey brings them together by considering how students learn to transfer their knowledge of writing to new situations. Her reflective model is premised on what she calls JIT transfer (just-in-time transfer), where students draw on skills and strategies from past experiences through school, internships, or day-to-day life when they recognize the need to do so. Her internship students found writing for an external audience completely different from writing in the classroom, and this called for them to reflect on what they learned through those past experiences and employ them in new ways.

I experienced this same roadblock when writing both stories in this portfolio. I had never made a magazine spread or a newspaper article, but the assignments called for me to be able to do so. The Seahawks spread required me to draw from the sports writing I had been reading throughout my day-to-day life, knowing that I would be writing original work about a topic that could hold up to countless professional writers in the genre who had written about the same subject. The interview with Schrader required me to draw from the genre of literary journalism and true crime that I know from podcasts and documentaries, not from my academic education. In both situations, I was doing what Yancey preached, borrowing from other contexts to find the right tool for the rhetorical job.

The concept map in this portfolio illustrates that process. It's not a professional product like the other two pieces, but a mind map needing me to employ that JIT transfer in the classroom mindset — a waymap of ideas in progress — and including it is itself a Yancey reflective gesture, mapping the thinking behind the finished product (Yancey 4).

Conclusion

What ties these three authors together is a concern that digital writing is never neutral, with meaning behind every word, socially and rhetorically, whether the author meant it to be that way or not. Craig says the platform shapes what counts as evidence. Babb says the community shapes what an identity comes to mean. Yancey says the writer chooses to learn from that process. My ePortfolio is a space to reflect on all three. To curate, to perform genre, and to reflect on why I am choosing to put my name on these pieces in each unfamiliar genre.

The Seahawks' spread is homegrown, just like the piece (Seattle born and raised). The interview with Schrader is more about digging into Vince Gilligan's direction and buildup of every character in Breaking Bad. The mind map is to test how I can connect works from different authors by employing the methods they wrote about and practicing what they preach. The skills that went into creating these pieces helped me further my skills that I will use for the rest of my life.
